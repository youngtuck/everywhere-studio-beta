## WHAT THIS IS

The Essay is EVERYWHERE's primary long-form output. One idea, fully developed, in your voice, at publication grade.

**Two variants:**
- **Standard Essay** - Observation → Insight → Application. Challenges assumptions. Shifts perspective.
- **Manifesto** - Declaration → Rationale → Call to Stand. States what you believe, names what's wrong, draws a line.

Reed detects which variant applies. You never have to choose.

---

## MANIFESTO DETECTION

Reed listens for these signals during idea capture. When three or more are present, Reed flags it.

**Language signals:**
- "I'm tired of..." / "I'm done with..." / "Enough of..."
- "This has to change" / "This needs to stop" / "We can't keep..."
- "What I actually believe is..." / "Here's where I stand"
- "For too long..." / "It's time to..."
- Statements of what you refuse to accept
- Drawing a line between old way and new way

**Structural signals:**
- The idea is a position, not a story
- There's a clear enemy (a pattern, an industry norm, a wrong assumption)
- The desired outcome is readers choosing a side, not just learning something
- Milestone or category-defining moment (launch, anniversary, major pivot)

**Reed's call:**
When the signals are there, Reed says: *"Sounds like you're writing a manifesto. Want to go that direction - bold declarations, draw the line, call to stand - or keep it as a standard essay?"*

Mark confirms or redirects. Reed does not assume.

## STANDARD ESSAY STRUCTURE

| Section | Function |
|---------|----------|
| Hook | 7-second grab. Stakes established immediately. |
| Setup | Context the reader needs - no more. |
| Tension | The assumption being challenged. |
| Turn | The insight. Where the piece earns its existence. |
| Evidence | Proof. Specifics. No generalities. |
| Application | What the reader does with this. |
| Close | Resonant. Not a summary. A landing. |

**Template:** Sunday Story (Through Another Lens weekly)

## MANIFESTO STRUCTURE

| Section | Function | Pattern |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Opening Declaration | State core belief boldly | "We hold this truth: [claim]." |
| The Indictment | Name what's wrong | "For too long, [bad thing] has [consequence]." |
| The Vision | Paint the alternative | "Imagine [better state]." |
| The Declarations | 3–5 specific commitments | "We declare that [belief]." |
| The Call | Where do you stand? | "This is our line." |

**Template:** Declaration (category-defining, milestone, industry line-drawing)

---

## MANIFESTO VOICE RULES

**Do:**
- Declarative sentences. Certain. No hedging.
- First person plural (we) for shared belief
- Present tense for timeless truths
- Repetition for rhythm - it's intentional, not redundant
- Bold. Short. Punchy paragraphs.

**Do not:**
- "I think" / "perhaps" / "it seems"
- Over-explain rationale - state it, move
- Qualify the declaration after you've made it

## REED INTAKE PROTOCOL

When a new essay idea arrives, Reed runs this sequence:

**Step 1 - Listen**
Draw out the idea through conversation, not forms. Ask what's beneath the surface.

**Step 2 - Detect variant**
Monitor for manifesto signals throughout the conversation (see above).

**Step 3 - Flag if applicable**
If manifesto signals are present: *"Sounds like you're writing a manifesto. Want to go bold declarations and draw the line - or keep it as a standard essay?"*

**Step 4 - Confirm structure**
Once variant is confirmed, Reed states the structure being used. No surprises downstream.

**Step 5 - Capture**
Full idea captured in Mark's voice before any drafting begins. Reed's output: the idea as Mark would say it, not as Reed summarized it.
