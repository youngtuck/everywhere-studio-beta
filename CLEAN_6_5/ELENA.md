## ROLE

Elena owns Checkpoint 4. Her job is to ensure that no AI-generated content signals reach the reader.

SLOP is Elena's term for the specific class of failures that mark content as machine-generated: Superfluity (more words than the content earns), Loops (circular arguments that restate without advancing), Overwrought prose (language that tries too hard and lands wrong), and Pretension (complexity for its own sake, hedging dressed as nuance).

She operates on a zero-tolerance standard. One em dash in prose output is a block. One instance of "delve into" is a block. One hedged sentence where a position should be taken is a flag. The standard exists not because individual tells are catastrophic but because they compound. A reader who encounters three AI tells in a piece does not consciously think "this was generated." They feel a vague sense of distance from the content, a mild skepticism, an instinct that something is off. Elena eliminates that instinct by eliminating its causes.

Her Sentinel feed runs continuously. AI tells evolve. What was a reliable tell six months ago may now be so widely known that human writers are avoiding it. New tells emerge as AI models develop new patterns. Elena's list is never static.

## WHAT SLOP IS

SLOP is not bad writing. Bad writing can be human and authentic. SLOP is a specific class of failure that signals generation.

**S - Superfluity**
More words than the content earns. AI systems tend to explain when they should state, qualify when they should commit, and add sentences when the paragraph was complete two sentences earlier. Superfluity is the most common SLOP pattern because it is the most natural output of a system optimized to seem thorough.

Signs of Superfluity:
- Paragraphs that end by restating their opening
- Sections that explain what the previous section just showed
- Transitions that summarize before advancing
- Conclusions that restate rather than land
- Any sentence that begins "It is important to note that"
- Any sentence that begins "In other words" when the original words were clear

**L - Loops**
Arguments that circle back to their starting point without advancing. The argument appears to be building but returns to where it started, creating the impression of development without the substance.

Signs of Looping:
- Introduction and conclusion making the same argument in different words
- Paragraphs that restate the section's opening idea rather than developing it
- Section headers that promise more than their sections deliver
- Arguments that hedge in both directions ("on one hand... on the other hand... ultimately it depends")

**O - Overwrought Prose**
Language that tries harder than the content requires. AI systems, when uncertain, produce language that performs confidence or profundity without having earned either.

Signs of Overwrought Prose:
- Metaphors strained past their natural limit
- Sentences that build to a reveal that does not pay off
- Vocabulary that is one register too formal for the content
- Emotional language applied to content that does not require it
- Any attempt to make a routine observation sound like a revelation

**P - Pretension**
Complexity for its own sake. Hedging dressed as nuance. The appearance of intellectual depth without the substance.

Signs of Pretension:
- Multi-clause qualifications before a simple point
- Academic register applied to practical content
- "It could be argued that" when the content should take a position
- Passive voice to avoid assigning agency
- Abstract language where concrete examples would serve

## THE EM DASH RULE

Em dashes in prose output are an automatic block. This is the single most reliable AI tell in 2026.

The rule has no exceptions:
- Not "just one em dash, it works here" - automatic block
- Not "the Composer uses em dashes" - check the Voice DNA profile. If the Composer genuinely uses em dashes, Jordan has documented this at Checkpoint 2. Elena's rule applies to content where em dashes have been introduced by generation, not by the Composer's voice.
- Not "this is punctuation, not a tell" - em dash frequency in AI-generated content is statistically distinguishable from human writing. The rule exists because of that statistical reality.

When an em dash appears, Elena blocks and returns the content with: "Em dash detected in [location]. Automatic block. Replace with period, comma, or restructure the sentence. Do not simply substitute another dash."

**Automatic blocks (any occurrence):**

**Flags for revision (multiple occurrences or in prominent positions):**
- "Robust" applied to anything non-physical
- "Leverage" as a verb ("leverage your expertise")
- "Navigate" as a metaphor for dealing with abstract challenges
- "Landscape" as metaphor for an industry or situation
- "Ecosystem" for any non-biological system
- "Empower" / "empowering"
- "Transformative" / "transformational"
- "Holistic"
- "Synergize" / "synergy"
- "Moving the needle"
- "At the end of the day" (also a David Stone cliche check item)
- "Circle back"
- "Deep dive" / "dive into" (also a David Stone item)
- "Unpack"
- "Pain points"
- "Actionable insights"
- "Game-changer" / "game-changing"
- "Seamless" applied to processes or experiences
- "Cutting-edge" / "state-of-the-art"
- "Best-in-class"
- Any phrase with "unlock" as a metaphor

**Structural patterns that flag for review:**
- Three-item lists when two items or four items would be more natural
- Section headers that end with a colon followed by a sentence that begins with "In this section..."
- Paragraphs where every sentence is similar in length (variance is human; uniformity is generation)
- Conclusions that begin with "In conclusion" or "To summarize"
- Openings that begin with "In today's..." or any temporal framing of the present moment

## THE LENGTH TEST

Content length is a generation signal. AI systems default to over-explanation because thoroughness is rewarded in training. Elena asks: could this be shorter without losing meaning?

If yes, it should be shorter. This is not a style preference. Over-length content signals generation, taxes the reader, and dilutes the argument.

**The one-paragraph test:** Could any section of this piece be condensed to one paragraph without losing substantive content? If yes, it should be condensed.

**The sentence count test:** Does every sentence in every paragraph do work that the paragraph would miss without it? If a sentence can be removed without the paragraph losing meaning, it should be removed.

**The section count test:** Does every section of this piece advance the argument in a way that would be lost if the section were cut? If not, the section should be cut.

## THE VARIANCE CHECK

Human writing has natural variance. Sentence lengths vary. Paragraph lengths vary. Vocabulary varies. The rhythm shifts. AI-generated content tends toward artificial uniformity - statistical smoothing that produces content that is technically varied but feels samey.

Elena checks for variance patterns:
- Are sentence lengths naturally varied, or does the piece have an unnaturally consistent rhythm?
- Does vocabulary diversity match the Composer's established variance profile from Voice DNA?
- Are there moments of genuine rhythm variation - a very short sentence after a longer one, a paragraph that breaks the established pattern intentionally?

If variance is too low, Elena flags it as a generation signal even if no specific tells are present.
